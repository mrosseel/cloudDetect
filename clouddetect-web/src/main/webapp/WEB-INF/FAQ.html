What is the purpose of this website?

A. Cloudwatch.net wants to gather real-time measurements of cloud status (clear, partially clear, cloudy) at night. Amateur astronomers (and possibly others) can then monitor the cloud status in their city/region and take appropriate action such as going out to observe, close the observatory during a remote imaging session, ...

How is the cloud cover measured?

A. In principle, cloudwatch.net doesn't care how the cloud coverage is measured, all (correct) methods are welcomed. At the moment we support the in-house developed low-cost webcam solution, but as soon as someone offers to put his Boltwood Sky Sensor measurements online that will be supported as well.

What's this low cost webcam solution you talk about?

A. Since the only commercially available cloud sensor is kinda pricey, a low cost webcam method was developed and tested. In short: if you can get an image from your webcam online, we can measure the cloud cover it sees. The good thing about it is that you don't need a PC running 24/7 (more about that at the webcam solution pages).

Why would I share my cloud cover results?

A. Whether you have a commercial sky sensor or a webcam solution, sharing is the main goal of this website. Besides the fact that it should make you feel good, publishing your results enables less equiped astronomers in your neighbourhood to enhance their hobby. What's in it for you? The cloud cover is calculated for you and presented in a nice graph, notifications, statistics, ... . People providing their measurements will also have a special place in my heart, possibly resulting in some extra features for them. Plus, when you're on a trip/renting a remote observatory, maybe someone has published his results and you can benefit from them.

Can I get your software/algorithms and do this offline?

A. In order for this website to be useful, I would really love it if you shared your results! If you really don't have the bandwidth, you could find someone in the neighbourhood who does. There are no secrets though, so I can explain you the algorithm if you want to reimplement the webcam solution. I'm not opposed to open sourcing the software, but it's kinda low on the priority list at the moment.

Can you implement feature X/Y?

A. Time is limited so not all feature requests will be implemented, but I value the input!

You will eat all my bandwidth!!

A. Calm down! A 640x480 image with low jpeg compression is only 64Kb. Suppose I poll your webcam every 5 minutes for one month. This results in an average upload of 548 Mb per month. If I poll every minute it's 2.7 Gb per month. In most parts of the world, these are reasonable bandwidths. I'm also looking into limiting the upload to the night only, slashing these figures at least by half. The webcam image itself can be requested from cloudwatch.net by users, but it's not refreshed automatically and put on a non-bookmarkable page to limit traffic (disabling this view might be implemented in a later stage).

You will be ruined by bandwidth costs!

A. Possibly...I've tried to limit the bandwidth usage to a minimum. Ideally I would run this in a data center with lots of bandwidth. In order to finance this I'm considering an add-supported free version and an ad-free subscription version. I'll try to give the free version as much features as possible, the really bandwidth-hungry features will be reserved for the subscription version. It's a balance which will have to be figured out as we go.

What's in the future?

A. With a decent grid of measuring stations, you could predict when clouds will arrive at your location (wind direction + stations downwind). I could implement SMS services to notify you. Enterprising hardware people could develop an interface between the website and your observatory roll-off roof, so it can open/close automatically. Statistics about average cloud cover for your site could be calculated, etc ...
